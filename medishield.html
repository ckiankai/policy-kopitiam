the baseline layer

MediShield Life

A neutral explainer of Singapore’s national health insurance, written to help you understand the mechanics before you buy anything else.

Educational only. Not advice. No endorsements. No insurer affiliations.

A simple idea: MediShield Life is designed to stop a medical bill from wrecking your life plans, not to make healthcare feel luxurious.

Tip: use “Contents” (bottom-right) to jump around.

What it is, in one minute

MediShield Life is Singapore’s basic national health insurance. It helps pay for large hospital bills and selected costly outpatient treatments, so you use less MediSave or cash. The key word is large, it is not meant to cover everything, and it is not meant to make healthcare feel free.

Who is covered?

All Singapore Citizens and Permanent Residents are automatically covered, and coverage continues regardless of age or health conditions.

Floor, not ceiling

If you understand MediShield Life first, everything else becomes easier. Integrated Shield Plans and riders are best understood as optional layers that modify gaps or comfort, they are not “replacements” for the base layer.

Why MediShield Life exists

Serious illness is both a health shock and a financial shock. Bills can be large, sudden, and poorly timed, especially if the illness reduces your ability to work. MediShield Life exists to reduce the chance that one medical episode becomes long-term financial damage.

The problem it solves

A rare event for an individual can still be common across a population. If costs are concentrated on the unlucky few, the outcome can be catastrophic.

MediShield Life spreads that risk so that the cost of bad luck is shared across many people over time. This is the logic of social insurance.

What it is not trying to do

It is not trying to eliminate every out-of-pocket dollar. It is not trying to subsidise comfort preferences for everyone.

If it tried to cover everything, premiums would rise sharply for everyone, including those who can least afford it.

A national scheme usually draws a line between medical necessity and personal preference. MediShield Life sits on the necessity side of that line.

The baseline is built on
subsidised care in public hospitals

MediShield Life benefits are sized around subsidised treatment in public hospitals. This is why ward classes like B2 or C keep showing up in discussions, they are used as the benchmark when sizing payouts.

If you choose higher comfort

You are still covered, but MediShield Life usually covers a smaller share of the total bill. The remainder is paid using MediSave and cash.

This is the practical origin of “gaps”, not because MediShield is broken, but because the bill structure changed.

This is intentional

Comfort is treated as a personal preference. The national scheme is designed to make necessary care financially survivable, not to guarantee private-room comfort by default.

A useful anchor: “subsidised first, everything else is optional layers”.

What it covers, and what it does not

A lot of confusion disappears once you accept this, MediShield Life is designed to cover medical necessity, not daily healthcare, and not lifestyle preferences.

Designed to cover

big, necessary medical expenses

  • large hospital bills, inpatient stays
  • day surgery
  • selected costly outpatient treatments (the kind that can run for months)

Not designed to cover

things that would make the scheme unaffordable if fully socialised

  • routine GP visits and common medications
  • elective or cosmetic procedures
  • comfort upgrades as a default
  • every dollar of private hospital treatment

If something feels excluded, ask whether it is a medical necessity problem or a comfort choice problem. MediShield Life focuses on necessity, anything beyond that is usually handled by optional layers.

Claim limits, deductibles and co-insurance

MediShield Life uses cost-sharing. That means you usually pay part of the bill even when insured. This is not a flaw, it is how the scheme keeps premiums sustainable and usage sensible.

Maximum claim limits

MediShield Life has claim limits by service type and stay length, and an overall cap, the maximum claim limit per policy year is $200,000, and there is no lifetime limit.

Deductible

The deductible is the amount you pay first in a policy year before MediShield Life starts paying.

Think of it as a threshold that filters out smaller bills, and focuses insurance on large, disruptive ones.

Co-insurance

After the deductible, you still pay a percentage of the remaining claimable amount. This is the co-insurance portion.

The point is not to punish patients, it is to avoid the system behaving like “free consumption”, which tends to push costs up for everyone.

A practical way to think about it: MediShield Life is meant to protect you from the tail risk, the huge bills, not to remove every bit of discomfort from the process.

Why coverage is lifelong

Health risks rise with age, and so do medical costs. If coverage ended at older ages, insurance would disappear exactly when people need it most. MediShield Life solves this by being lifelong, it continues regardless of age or health status.

Universal means no cherry picking

Everyone is in, including those with pre-existing conditions. That is part of the social contract behind a national scheme.

Lifelong does not mean free

Premiums reflect risk, and risk rises with age. The point is not to make costs disappear, it is to keep coverage available for life.

How premiums and claims usually flow

You don’t need to memorise every administrative step, but it helps to understand the overall flow, because it clarifies where “out of pocket” comes from.

Premiums

Premiums are typically deducted automatically from MediSave. If MediSave is insufficient, you can top up, and family members can help pay using their MediSave.

Claims

In most cases the hospital submits the claim for you. If you also have an Integrated Shield Plan, your insurer usually coordinates the total payment, including the MediShield Life portion.

The remainder is settled via MediSave and or cash.

What matters is knowing which layer pays what, and why the gap exists, not becoming an expert in paperwork.

How to check your MediShield Life

If you want to sanity check what you have, start with official portals linked to Singpass and CPF services. You should be able to view your coverage status, premium information, and claim records.

What to look for

  • coverage status, active or not
  • premium amount and payment method
  • claim history entries
  • whether an Integrated Shield Plan is linked on top

Small tip: always anchor on MediShield Life first, it prevents you from paying twice for the same baseline layer

Integrated Shield Plans (ISPs)

Integrated Shield Plans are optional private insurance plans that sit on top of MediShield Life. They do not replace MediShield Life, instead, they build on it.

MediShield Life provides a baseline level of protection, focused on subsidised care and large bills. An Integrated Shield Plan typically increases coverage limits and reduces gaps, especially when you choose higher ward classes or private hospitals.

What ISPs change

  • higher claim limits
  • less pro-ration for private or non-subsidised care
  • more predictable out-of-pocket costs (with riders)

What ISPs do not change

  • MediShield Life still forms the base layer
  • deductibles and co-insurance still exist
  • cost-sharing does not disappear by default

Whether an Integrated Shield Plan makes sense depends on your preferences for hospital choice, cost certainty, and long-term premiums, rather than on medical necessity alone.

Glossary

You will see some terms underlined on this page. Hover on desktop or tap on mobile for a quick definition.

MediShield Life

Singapore’s basic national health insurance for large hospital bills and selected costly outpatient treatments.

Deductible

The fixed amount you pay first before insurance starts paying.

Co-insurance

The percentage you pay after deductible, the insurer pays the rest (simplified).

Claim limit

A cap on how much can be claimed for specific services, meant to keep the scheme sustainable.

Subsidised ward benchmark

The reference point used when sizing MediShield Life benefits, often discussed as B2 or C in public hospitals.

Integrated Shield Plan

Optional private insurance that sits on top of MediShield Life to reduce gaps, usually for higher ward classes or private hospitals.
