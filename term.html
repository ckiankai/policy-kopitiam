Term insurance

What it is, in one minute

Term insurance provides coverage for a fixed period (the “term”). If a covered event happens during that period, it pays a lump sum. If the term ends and nothing happens, the coverage ends too.

Three moving parts

  • Sum assured, how much is paid if the trigger happens
  • Policy term, how long you are covered for
  • Premium, what you pay to keep coverage active

The “no payout at the end” part is not a bug, it’s the point, you’re buying protection, not returns.

Quiet reminder: term insurance is about the people and obligations attached to you, not about paying hospitals (that’s MediShield Life and ISPs!)

Claim triggers, what actually makes it pay

Life insurance doesn’t pay because you’re “unwell”. It pays when a specific, defined event happens. These triggers are the common ones you’ll see across term, whole life, and ILPs.

Death

a lump sum paid to beneficiaries

  • protects dependants and household cashflow
  • can cover liabilities like a mortgage
  • may trigger even if there is no major medical bill

Total & Permanent Disability (TPD)

a severe, permanent loss of ability to work (policy-defined)

  • meant for long-run income loss, not short-term injury
  • definitions matter a lot, always check the product summary
  • pays cash, hospital insurance is a separate layer

Critical Illness (CI)

a diagnosis-based trigger (policy-defined list and severity)

  • pays a lump sum on diagnosis of a covered condition
  • often meant to support time off work and non-medical costs
  • does not replace hospital insurance, it complements it (different job)
More triggers = more ways the policy can pay out = usually a higher premium

Why it exists, beyond hospital bills

MediShield Life and ISPs are mainly triggered by eligible medical expenses. Term insurance is mainly triggered by life events that disrupt earning capacity and dependency.

Hospital insurance

“a medical bill exists, who pays it?”

This is MediShield Life and ISPs. It’s about claim limits, deductibles, co-insurance, and hospital setting choices.

Life protection

“income stops, what happens to the people relying on it?”

This is term, whole life, and ILPs. It’s about dependants, liabilities, and preserving a life plan when luck goes bad.

Simple example: a sudden death may involve little or no hospital bill - hospital insurance does nothing here, life protection is the relevant layer

Premium mechanics, why it costs what it costs

A clean mental model: premium is the price of risk transfer. Anything that increases the insurer’s expected payout usually increases premium.

Things that usually increase premium

  • higher sum assured, bigger potential payout
  • older age at entry, higher probability of the trigger happening
  • longer policy term, more years exposed to risk
  • smoker, higher statistical risk
  • more triggers, e.g. adding CI/TPD on top of death

Things that change the feel (not just the price)

  • definitions and exclusions, what counts as a valid claim
  • renewability and convertibility, what happens after the term ends
  • how you plan to invest separately (if you choose term)
  • whether your “need” is temporary or lifelong
Higher risk, higher payout potential, or more ways to claim, usually means higher premiums

Strengths and weaknesses

Term insurance is the simplest version of life protection: you pay a premium for a fixed period, and it pays out if a defined trigger happens during that period. The simplicity is the point, but it also means term has a clear boundary, it only solves protection.

Strengths

  • usually the lowest cost per dollar of protection
  • clean product logic, pay for risk transfer, not savings
  • easy to size to a responsibility window (kids, mortgage, income replacement)
  • easy to adjust over time, because you are not unwinding a long-horizon structure

Term feels “efficient” when you can name the window. It’s like renting protection for the years you actually need it.

Weaknesses

  • coverage expires by design, no payout if nothing happens
  • renewing later can be more expensive
  • if you keep delaying, you may price yourself out
  • it doesn’t force savings or investing discipline

Term feels “painful” when someone secretly wants it to behave like a savings product. It won’t, and it’s not meant to.

Compared to whole life

  • cheaper premiums for the same sum assured
  • more flexible, easier to change plans later
  • but expires, whole life is built to persist

If whole life is “lock it in for life”, term is “cover the window, then reassess”.

Compared to ILPs

  • simpler and usually cheaper for pure protection
  • no market-linked account value, no investment volatility
  • but you must invest separately if you want growth

Term keeps the jobs clean: insurance does insurance.

Quick anchor

  • term solves a time window
  • whole life solves permanence
  • ILPs solve growth + flexibility, at the cost of certainty

the simplest tool that solves the job is often the best starting point

Practical rule of thumb: if you can invest consistently without needing a product to force you, many people choose term for protection and invest separately. If you want the policy to carry discipline for you, that’s where whole life or ILPs enter the conversation.

Term is protection first, if you want growth, build growth intentionally elsewhere

Before you buy

Term is straightforward, but “straightforward” doesn’t mean “mindless”. The goal is to buy coverage that matches the responsibility window, without turning premiums into stress later.

Fit questions

  • who is financially affected if my income stops
  • what window am i protecting, until kids are independent, until mortgage is paid
  • is this replacing income, covering liabilities, or both
  • what coverage do i already have (employer, CPF, existing policies)

Mechanics questions

  • what exactly triggers the payout (death, TPD, CI), and how are they defined
  • what are the key exclusions
  • is the premium level or renewable, what happens after the term ends
  • can it be converted later, and under what conditions

Small habit that helps: ask to see the actual definitions in the product summary, not only brochure phrasing

Common mistakes

Most mistakes here come from mismatching the product to the problem. These are common friction points, not personal failures.

Buying too short a term

coverage ends while dependants or liabilities still exist

Buying coverage without naming the target

if you can’t name who or what you’re protecting, you may be buying anxiety relief, not protection

Assuming hospital insurance replaces income

MediShield/ISP pays medical bills, term/whole/ILP pays people, different job

Delaying until it becomes expensive

premiums tend to rise with age and health changes, earlier is often cheaper for the same specs

Know what you’re protecting first, then choose the simplest tool that does that job!

Glossary

Hover on desktop or tap on mobile for underlined terms. Here are the ones that keep showing up when people compare term, whole life, and ILPs.

Sum assured

The amount paid out if the claim trigger happens during the coverage period.

Premium

The amount you pay (monthly or yearly) to keep coverage active.

Policy term

How long the coverage lasts (e.g. 20 years or to age 65).

Exclusion

Situations or conditions the policy does not cover, always check the policy definition.

TPD

Total and Permanent Disability, severe and permanent disability as defined by the policy.

CI

Critical Illness, a diagnosis-based trigger for covered conditions and definitions.

Renewable / Convertible

Features that allow extension or conversion later, terms and conditions vary across products.

Rider

An add-on that modifies or extends a base policy, subject to policy terms.

What “enough” looks like

For term insurance, “enough” is usually not “maximum coverage”. It’s “if something happens during this window, the people relying on me can keep going without a financial spiral”.

Term insurance is a way to make sure a specific period of responsibility doesn’t become a permanent setback for others.
