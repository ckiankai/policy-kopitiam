Investment-linked Policies

(ILPs)

Investment-Linked Policies bundle insurance protection with market investments. Returns are not guaranteed, outcomes depend heavily on markets, charges, and behaviour.

Educational only. Not advice. No endorsements. No insurer affiliations.

A simple idea: ILPs trade certainty for market participation, and trade simplicity for flexibility.

Tip: use “Contents” (bottom-right) to jump around.

What it is, in one minute

An Investment-Linked Policy (ILP) is a life insurance policy where part of your premium goes to insurance charges, and part is used to buy investment units in funds you choose.

Three moving parts

  • Protection, what triggers payout (death, TPD, and CI, depending on the policy)
  • Investments, units in funds that rise and fall with markets
  • Charges, costs deducted along the way (not just once)

The investment risk sits with you, not the insurer. When markets fall, your account value can fall too.

What ILPs are good at

  • bundling “invest + insure” into one ongoing habit
  • flexibility to adjust premiums, coverage, or funds (product-specific)
  • market exposure over long horizons, if you stay consistent

Where ILPs bite

  • charges compound over time, not always obvious upfront
  • early exits can be painful (surrender penalties, low value)
  • behaviour matters, switching, stopping, panic decisions

Same claim triggers exist across term, whole life, and ILPs. The difference is what happens to the “savings / investing” side, and who bears the investment risk.

How ILPs grow, and don’t

People usually imagine an ILP as “premium in, value up”. In reality, it’s closer to “premium in, charges out, units bought, markets move”.

Growth comes from markets

  • your account value depends on fund performance
  • returns are not promised, projections are not guarantees
  • bad sequences early can take longer to recover

“Good long-term market returns” is a real force, but it does not rescue every structure or every behaviour.

Friction comes from charges

  • charges reduce how much actually gets invested
  • insurance costs often rise as you age
  • small percentages compound into real money over decades

In ILPs, returns have to overcome friction first.

Markets drive the engine, charges are the drag, behaviour is the steering

Practical mental model: an ILP is not a “bad product” or “good product”. it is a product where outcomes are sensitive to assumptions and actions.

Charges, the silent driver

If you ignore charges, ILPs can look simpler than they are. Charges are not “evil”, they’re the cost of running the policy and providing protection, but they meaningfully change long-run outcomes.

Common charge buckets

  • insurance charges, cost of coverage, often rises with age
  • policy / admin charges, the platform cost of the policy
  • fund-level fees, ongoing management costs inside funds
  • other structure items, varies by product (ask what applies)

Why this matters

  • charges reduce net growth over decades
  • “small %” compounds into large $ differences
  • early years can feel slow because costs are front-loaded or heavy
  • premium increases later may be needed to sustain coverage

A useful habit: ask for a clean summary of ongoing charges and what happens in the first 5 years vs 20 years. Not just the projected returns page.

What breaks if…

These are not “gotchas”. They are predictable consequences of how ILPs are structured and used.

Common ILP pain points (accordion)

…markets fall early in the policy

early losses can feel more painful because your account value is still small, charges still apply, and recovery needs time.

key idea, sequence matters, bad early years can create a slow-feeling start even if long-run markets recover

…i stop paying premiums for a while

the policy may continue by deducting charges from your account value, but if value becomes insufficient, the policy can lapse, depending on terms.

key idea, the insurance charges don’t pause just because your cashflow paused

…i surrender early because i’m disappointed

early surrender can lock in losses and may trigger penalties or poor value relative to what you paid, especially if the structure is front-loaded.

key idea, ILPs reward staying, early exits can feel harsh because the product assumes long holding periods

…i keep switching funds based on recent performance

frequent switching can turn long-term investing into short-term guessing. Even without explicit switching fees, the behaviour risk is real.

key idea, a market-linked product is already volatile, adding timing decisions can amplify the volatility you personally experience

…i read projected returns as guaranteed outcomes

projections are not promises, they are scenarios. what matters is the structure, the charges, and your ability to stay invested.

key idea, in ILPs, you should treat illustrations as “what could happen”, not “what will happen”

ILPs are not just a product, they are a behaviour contract

Strengths and weaknesses

Investment-linked policies combine protection with market participation. Their outcomes depend not just on markets, but on structure, charges, and how consistently the policy is held. ILPs are powerful when behaviour aligns, and unforgiving when it doesn’t.

Strengths

  • potential for market-linked growth over long horizons
  • flexibility to adjust funds, premiums, or coverage (product-dependent)
  • bundles investing and insurance into one ongoing habit
  • transparent link between markets and account value

ILPs feel “useful” when they help someone stay invested consistently, rather than trying to optimise returns.

Weaknesses

  • investment value is not guaranteed
  • charges compound and materially affect long-term outcomes
  • high behaviour risk, stopping or switching early hurts
  • early exit is often inefficient by design

ILPs feel “painful” when treated like a flexible savings account. They assume time, consistency, and discipline.

Compared to term

  • adds investing but also adds complexity and charges
  • term is usually cheaper for pure protection
  • ILPs work best when held consistently

ILPs trade simplicity for bundling and growth potential.

Compared to whole life

  • no guaranteed floor, values move with markets
  • greater flexibility and upside potential
  • less certainty in outcomes

ILPs trade guarantees for participation.

Quick anchor

  • term solves a window
  • whole life solves permanence
  • ILPs solve growth and flexibility

Product choice is also behaviour choice.

ILPs amplify both discipline and mistakes

A common recommendation: separate protection and investing

A neutral way to think about it: ILPs combine two jobs, protection and investing. Many people prefer separating these jobs so each can be evaluated clearly.

One common approach

  • buy term insurance for protection
  • invest separately through a broker or cash management platform

If you’re not lazy (meaning you can invest consistently without needing a product to force you), this separation often gives cleaner transparency and easier flexibility.

Why people do this

  • clear cost visibility, insurance cost stays insurance cost
  • more control over asset allocation and switching
  • easier exit, no need to “unwind a bundle”
  • less temptation to treat insurance as investment performance

This is not saying ILPs are always wrong. ILPs can help people who value automation, forced consistency, or who know they won’t invest otherwise. The question is what you need the product to do for your behaviour.

If you can invest consistently on your own, you usually don’t need to pay a structure to do it for you

Before you buy

ILPs are not “complicated” because of vocabulary. They are complicated because outcomes depend on assumptions, charges, and actions over time. The goal is to understand the structure before you commit to it.

Fit questions

  • am i buying this for protection, investing, or behaviour forcing
  • if markets fall 30%, will i stay consistent
  • am i likely to stop paying or surrender early
  • do i prefer transparency and separate accounts, or a bundled structure

Mechanics questions

  • what are all ongoing charges, and how do they change over time
  • how does insurance cost change as i age
  • what happens if i stop paying for 3–6 months
  • what are surrender terms in the first 5–10 years
  • what funds are available, and what are their fund-level fees

Small habit that helps: ask for an explanation of “what happens in year 3, year 8, year 20”, not just the best-looking projection page.

Glossary (ILP words)

This page avoids repeating the whole life / term glossary. Here are the ILP-specific words that keep showing up.

Account value

The value of your investment units after market movements and charges (simplified).

Units

The “shares” of the fund you hold inside the policy, their price moves with markets.

Fund fees

Ongoing costs inside the fund itself, separate from policy-level charges.

Insurance charges

The cost of the protection component, often increases with age.

Surrender

Ending the policy early, the amount you receive depends on value and terms (often painful early).

Illustration

A scenario projection, not a guaranteed outcome, use it to understand sensitivity.

What “enough” looks like

For ILPs, “enough” is usually not the highest projected return. It is understanding the structure, accepting volatility, and not abandoning the plan during stress.

ILPs can work, but only if you stay invested through downturns, understand the charges, and don’t treat projected returns as promises.
